I am a **Computer Engineer specialized in Robotics and Automation**, currently working in the field of **PLC software engineering** and **industrial automation**.

My professional focus is the development of reliable automation software for industrial systems, with particular attention to **PLC logic**, **motion control**, **HMI-oriented workflows**, **diagnostics**, **project structure** and **technical documentation**.

## Professional Profile

My work is driven by a practical engineering principle: automation software must be functional, but it must also be clear, traceable and maintainable.

In industrial environments, the value of software is not limited to the first successful execution. Good automation logic must remain understandable during commissioning, troubleshooting, maintenance and future upgrades.

For this reason, I focus on:

- clear separation between commands, feedbacks, alarms, states and interlocks;
- modular PLC software structure;
- coherent naming conventions;
- predictable operating states;
- readable diagnostics and operator-facing information;
- documentation that supports real project continuity.

## Industrial Automation Focus

I work mainly around PLC-based automation systems and engineering workflows connected to industrial control.

My areas of interest include:

- PLC software development;
- Siemens TIA Portal environments;
- S7-1200 and S7-1500 systems;
- HMI and WinCC-oriented workflows;
- motion control and axis management;
- servo drive integration concepts;
- industrial communication and signal mapping;
- commissioning-oriented software organization.

## Robotics and Control Background

My academic background in Computer Engineering, with specialization in Robotics and Automation, gave me a strong foundation in software development, control systems, robotics and system integration.

This background allows me to approach automation projects not only from the PLC implementation side, but also from a broader control-system perspective: understanding process behavior, constraints, feedbacks, interfaces and the impact of software architecture on system reliability.

## Technical Documentation and Standards

I consider documentation part of the engineering work, not an accessory after implementation.

I prefer documentation that is:

- practical;
- structured;
- easy to update;
- aligned with the actual project architecture;
- useful for maintenance and future development.

This includes technical guides, project standards, naming conventions, Markdown/LaTeX documentation, repository organization and reusable engineering templates.

## Working Style

I like structured and incremental workflows.

Before implementing, I prefer to clarify the process, define the logical architecture and identify the single source of truth for data, commands, feedbacks and states. This helps reduce ambiguity and keeps the project easier to test, maintain and evolve.

My preferred working style is based on:

- clear project structure;
- technical consistency;
- practical standards;
- readable logic;
- incremental improvement;
- documentation that remains useful after delivery.
